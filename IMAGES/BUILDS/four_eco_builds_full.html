Eco mushroom playhouse

Fully compostable at end of life. Every component has options — choose what you can get hold of most easily. Floor, cap skin, and hoop frame all have alternatives listed. Big enough for two kids, simple enough for one weekend.

Fully compostable ~$50–70 One weekend Ages 3+
Every single component composts. When the playhouse has had its day, pull it apart — stakes, rope, hessian, paper, canvas, and wood all break down naturally. The only things to keep and reuse are the strap hinges on the door.

Dimensions

  • Cap diameter: ~5 ft
  • Stem height: ~3½ ft
  • Door opening: 2 ft wide × 2½ ft tall
  • Floor: ~4 ft across
  • Interior: 2 kids comfortable

Weekend schedule

  • Saturday AM — floor + stake frame
  • Saturday PM — rope stem + cap frame
  • Saturday eve — cap skin layer 1
  • Sunday AM — layer 2 + spots + paint
  • Sunday PM — linseed oil seal + decorate

Key choices to make

  • Floor: pallets, cedar decking, plywood, or OSB
  • Cap hoops: screen moulding, garden edging, or bamboo stakes
  • Cap skin: papier-mâché (standard) or canvas (weatherproof)

Saturday morning — floor and frame
1

Build the floor

Choose the option that works best for you, then raise the finished floor 2–3 in off the ground on four flat stones — this is the single most important thing you can do for longevity.

Most eco — free if you can find them

Reclaimed wooden pallets

Break down 2–3 pallets and lay boards side by side to form a ~4 ft circle. Screw cross-battens underneath. Check Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, local garden centres, nurseries, or liquor stores — most give them away free.

Best bought option — naturally rot-resistant

Cedar decking boards

Buy cedar decking boards by the linear foot from Home Depot. Lay side by side to form a ~4 ft circle and screw cross-battens underneath. Naturally rot-resistant and fully compostable. Costs around $20–25.

Simplest single-piece option

Plywood sheet

Buy one sheet of ¾ in plywood (4×8 ft) and ask Home Depot to cut it into a rough circle in store. Costs around $30–40.

Cheapest option

OSB (oriented strand board)

One sheet of ¾ in OSB cut to a circle works perfectly. About half the price of plywood at around $15–20. Seal the top surface with linseed oil to slow moisture absorption.

2

Plant the upright stakes

Buy 8–10 thick wooden garden stakes (6 ft tall, 1½ in thick) from any hardware or garden shop. Or cut straight branches from a hazel or elder hedge. Drill holes around the floor perimeter and push stakes in about 8 in, screwing through a batten underneath to anchor them. Leave a 2 ft gap on one side for the door.

3

Lash a top ring to the stakes

Bend a long thin branch or soaked screen moulding strip into a circle at the top of the stakes, about 3½ ft from the ground. Lash it to each stake with jute twine — this ties all the uprights together and defines the stem shape.

Saturday afternoon — stem walls and cap frame
4

Coil jute rope around the stem

Starting at the base, wrap thick jute rope tightly around all the stakes going round and round upward like a coil pot. Dab wood glue or thick flour paste between each coil to bond them. Leave the 2 ft door gap open as you go.

Kids wind the rope round and round
5

Make and hang the door

Cut a door panel from reclaimed pallet boards or offcuts — a simple arched shape, slightly smaller than the 2 ft wide × 2½ ft tall gap. Screw a wooden batten vertically to one edge of the gap as the door post. Fix the door with two strap hinges. Add a jute rope loop through a drilled hole as the handle. The door swings outward.

6

Build the cap hoop frame

Soak 4 screen moulding strips (or thin bamboo garden stakes, or wood garden edging strips) in warm water for 20–30 minutes. Bend two into hoops ~3–3½ ft diameter and lash ends with jute twine. Stand these upright at 90° to each other, cross at the top, lash firmly. Push the bottoms out to ~5 ft and lash to the stem's top ring. Add a third hoop horizontally around the middle. Push the top point as high as possible — aim for at least 2 ft above the brim for good rain runoff.

Saturday afternoon — cap skin (choose your method)
7a

Drape hessian over the cap frame

Drape hessian sacking over the dome, cutting darts so it lies smooth. Tie to the top ring with jute twine. Kids can hold the fabric in place while you tie it off.

Kids hold and smooth the hessian
8a

Cook the flour paste

Mix 1 cup plain flour into 3 cups cold water until smooth, then heat gently stirring constantly until it thickens. Add a pinch of salt to deter mould. Leave to cool.

Kids stir the paste on the hob with supervision
9a

Papier-mâché layers 1 and 2

Tear brown paper bags, egg cartons, or cardboard packaging into palm-sized pieces. Dip in flour paste, squeeze off excess, and lay overlapping all over the hessian cap. Do two full layers, leaving overnight between them if possible.

Total kid takeover — messy and wonderful
7b

Stretch canvas over the cap frame

Cut heavy canvas or drop cloth to cover the 5 ft dome — allow an extra 6 in all around. Drape over the frame, pull taut, cut darts so it lies smooth. Staple or lash edges to the stem's top ring with jute twine. Should be drum-taut.

Why this works better: Canvas soaked in linseed oil forms a near-solid skin — the same material as traditional oilcloth. Rain beads and runs off immediately rather than soaking in.
8b

Soak canvas in raw linseed oil

Brush raw linseed oil over the entire canvas until saturated and dark. Leave 4–6 hours in sun. Apply a second coat once touch-dry. Canvas stiffens as it cures.

Kids brush on the oil with wide brushes
Safety: Lay linseed-soaked rags flat outdoors to dry — never pile them. They can self-combust.
9b

Wrap the stem in linseed-soaked burlap

Cut hessian into 3–4 in wide strips. Dip each in raw linseed oil and wrap tightly around the rope coil stem, overlapping each layer. Gives the stem a hard water-resistant shell. Leave to cure overnight.

Kids dip and hand strips up to wrap
Sunday morning — spots and paint
10

Make the white spots

Scrunch brown paper into tight balls ranging from golf-ball size to about 4 in across. Cover each in 2–3 layers of flour-paste papier-mâché and leave to dry hard and rigid. Glue onto the cap with thick flour paste or wood glue. Kids choose where every spot goes.

Kids choose all the spot positions
11

Paint with natural chalk or clay paint

Mix red ochre pigment powder into chalk paint for the cap. Use white chalk paint for the stem and spots. Two coats each. Once dry, brush a final coat of linseed oil over everything to lock the colour in and boost water resistance.

Big brushes, big paint, big fun
Sunday afternoon — finishing
12

Decorate and move in

Paint the door, press dried flowers into the last paste layer, add a hand-painted name sign, hang a wind chime from the cap rim. Add a rug from an old blanket and string up battery fairy lights. It's theirs now.

Completely their creative call

Steep dome = dry interior

Make the cap as tall and pointy as possible. Aim for at least 2 ft of height above the brim ring. A steep dome sheds rain in seconds — a shallow one pools water regardless of which skin method you use.

Raise the floor off the ground

Even just 2–3 in on flat stones keeps the base dry and can add years to the structure's life. This matters most for OSB and plywood — cedar and pallets are more forgiving but still benefit.

Linseed-oil-soaked rags can self-combust

Never bunch up linseed-soaked rags — lay them flat outdoors to dry completely before disposing. Keep children away. This is a genuine fire risk worth taking seriously.

Flour paste keeps 3 days in the fridge

Make a big batch Saturday morning and keep it covered. Still good Sunday for the second layer and the spots.

One linseed oil coat each spring

Takes 20 minutes and keeps the structure going indefinitely. Kids can brush it on themselves. In winter, throw an old canvas tarpaulin loosely over the top.

End of life — what to do with it

Remove and keep the strap hinges for reuse. Stakes, rope, hessian, and jute twine all go on the compost. Flour-paste papier-mâché dissolves in rain or breaks down in weeks. Canvas soaked in linseed oil biodegrades naturally. Cedar boards and pallet wood can be burned or composted. Plywood and OSB are best repurposed — donate or reuse as shelving or garden bed sides rather than composting due to their adhesive binders.

Six giant arches planted in a curve, each painted a different rainbow colour. Kids run through them, wildflowers bloom at the base all summer, and at night fairy lights make the whole yard glow in six colours. Visible from the end of the street.

~8 ft tall Fully compostable ~$40–55 4–5 hours total Ages 2+

1

Mark the rainbow curve

Lay a garden hose or string across your yard in a gentle curve. Space 6 marks along it roughly 2½ ft apart — each is the centre point between the two feet of one arch. A slight curve looks more natural and dramatic than a straight row.

2

Prepare your arch material

You need 6 pieces of flexible material, each at least 10–12 ft long. Screen moulding strips (¼ in × ¾ in pine, 8 ft lengths from Home Depot lumber aisle, ~$2 each — buy two and overlap for a 14 ft length) work best. Soak in warm water for 20–30 minutes before bending.

Double-up for strength: Use two strips side by side lashed with jute twine per arch leg. Lasts two to three seasons rather than one.
3

Plant all six arches

For each arch: push one end 12 in into the ground at your marked spot. Walk the strip over in a curve and push the other end 12 in into the ground about 5 ft away. It bends naturally into a perfect arch. If loose, push a second stake alongside the first leg. Repeat for all six.

Each kid paints their own arch

Assign one arch per child: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet. Mix red ochre pigment into chalk paint for warm colours. Two coats each from the ground up. The messier the brush strokes the better — it's a handmade rainbow.

Each kid fully owns their arch — no adult intervention needed

Seal with linseed oil

String the fairy lights

Kids wrap the lights — they are brilliant at this

Scatter wildflower seeds at the base

Double up the arch strips for longevity

A single screen moulding strip lasts one summer. Two strips lashed side by side lasts two to three seasons and feels much more solid in wind. The extra $2 per arch is absolutely worth it.

Push the feet in deep — 12 in minimum

The most common failure point is arches pulling out of the ground in wind. If soil is soft, push two stakes side by side for each foot, or tie a short cross-stake horizontally just below ground level to anchor it like a tent peg.

Wildflower seeds need scratched soil to germinate

Don't scatter on top of existing grass or hard ground — scratch the surface first with a fork to expose bare soil. This one step triples germination success. Water every day for the first two weeks.

Solar fairy lights mean zero effort all summer

Replace battery lights with solar-powered ones on a stake at the base of each arch. They charge during the day and switch on automatically at dusk — no effort all summer long.

One linseed oil coat each spring takes 10 minutes

The chalk paint fades gracefully over winter which looks beautiful — chalky and weathered. A fresh linseed coat revives the colours and adds another full season of life.

End of life — what to do with it

Pull the arch stakes and chop into short sections for the compost heap or bonfire. Jute twine composts completely. Wildflowers will self-seed for next year — let them. Remove battery packs and lights, store for the next project. The whole structure comes down in under 20 minutes and leaves nothing behind but a patch of self-seeding wildflowers.

Before you start — the gathering walk
1

Go on a family stick-gathering walk

Head to a park, woodland edge, or around your yard. You want thick branches (thumb-width, 4–6 ft long) for the main weave, and thinner flexible twigs for filling. Collect at least twice as much as you think you need. Let the kids lead — they will not stop.

Saturday morning — the frame
2

Mark the circle and plant frame stakes

Tie a 3 ft string to a centre stake. Walk the string taut in a circle and mark the perimeter — this is your 6 ft diameter circle. Hammer 8 thick garden stakes evenly around the circle, at least 12 in deep each, at a very slight outward angle to give the nest its bowl shape.

3

Weave the base layer

Take your longest flexible branches and weave them in and out between the upright stakes going around the full circle — in front of one stake, behind the next, exactly like basket weaving. Add rounds of thick jute rope between branch rows. Kids can do this entire stage after one demonstration.

Kids weave every branch — show the over-under pattern once and step back
Saturday afternoon — walls and filling
4

Build the walls to 18–20 in

Keep weaving until the walls reach 18–20 in — enough to feel enclosed while sitting inside but easy to climb in and out. The top doesn't need to be level or neat. A real nest is never neat.

Kids build the entire wall height
5

Pack in twigs, leaves, and small sticks

Give kids the entire pile of thin twigs, dried grass, and small sticks and let them tuck them into every gap in the weave — exactly as a real bird would. This fills holes, adds rigidity, and looks phenomenal. Do not interrupt them.

Pure kid job — give them the pile and step back completely
6

Line the inside and add lights

Lay hessian sacking across the inside floor. Add old cushions or a folded blanket. String battery fairy lights around the inside rim — tuck into the top layer of weaving so they glow through the sticks at night. Kids arrange the inside exactly as they want it.

Kids arrange the inside entirely their way

Make the giant papier-mâché egg

8

Pop, paint, and place the egg

Gather far more sticks than you think you need

The nest eats material. A pile that looks enormous will fill about two-thirds of the wall. Go back for more rather than running short mid-build — it kills momentum. Aim for a pile about 4 ft high before you start.

The messier it looks the better

Resist any urge to tidy or straighten the weave. Real nests are wild, chaotic, and uneven — that's exactly what makes them look authentic. If it looks too neat, pull a few sticks out at odd angles and push them back in randomly.

Plant the frame stakes at a slight outward angle

If you push all stakes perfectly vertical the nest will be a straight-sided cylinder. Angling each stake about 10° outward gives the nest a natural bowl curvature — wider at the top, narrower at the base.

Leave the egg to dry fully before popping the balloon

If you pop before fully dry the shell will flex and crack. Give it a full overnight dry minimum. Tap it — it should sound hollow and feel hard like a drum before you pop.

Leave it as a wildlife habitat at end of season

Before composting, leave the nest standing through autumn. Hedgehogs, beetles, and insects will move into the stick walls for winter. In late winter, pull it apart for the compost heap.

End of life — what to do with it

Leave standing through autumn as a wildlife habitat first. In late winter, pull the stakes and compost or bonfire. Every branch, twig, leaf, jute rope, and hessian piece composts completely. The papier-mâché egg dissolves in rain or breaks apart for the compost. Save the fairy lights for the next project. Zero waste — entirely back to the earth.

The living tunnel

Two rows of arched stakes lashed together at the top, seeded with climbing plants. Takes 2–3 hours to build. By midsummer it becomes a fully enclosed living green tunnel bursting with flowers — kids run through it, hide in it, and eat runner beans from it every night. Gets more beautiful every single week.

Saturday morning — the frame
1

Mark the two rows

Stretch two lengths of string parallel to each other, 3 ft apart and 15 ft long. Mark a stake position every 2 ft along each row — 8 positions per side, 16 total. Check the rows are parallel by measuring the gap at several points — 3 ft all the way along.

2

Hammer all the stakes

Hammer a 6 ft wooden garden stake at every marked position, pushing each in at least 12 in deep. Use the two tallest stakes (7–8 ft) for the entrance — the first pair at one end. Every stake should feel completely solid with no wobble. Pre-make a hole with a metal bar if the ground is hard.

3

Bend and lash the arches

Working pair by pair, gently push the top of the left stake toward the right stake until the tips meet at the centre, about 5–6 ft above the ground. Lash the two tips together firmly with jute twine — wrap at least 6 times and tie off tightly. Repeat for all 8 pairs. Then run a single jute rope along the full top ridge tying to each arch apex — this locks the whole tunnel into one rigid structure.

Saturday afternoon — string lines and floor
4

Tie all the horizontal string lines

Run lines of jute twine horizontally along both sides of the tunnel at roughly 8 in intervals from ground all the way up to the arch. Tie to each stake as you pass. Kids tie the lower lines (up to about 3 ft) while you do the higher ones.

Kids tie all lines from ground up to 3 ft
5

Frame the entrance arch

The first arch pair uses the two tallest stakes (7–8 ft) to create a more dramatic entrance. Lash a horizontal bar across the top between them — an old broomstick, thick branch, or offcut stake. Hang a papier-mâché sign, wind chime, or dried flower wreath from the bar.

6

Lay the floor path inside the tunnel

Kids lay a path along the inside floor between the two rows of stakes. Wood chip, gravel, flat stones, or large fallen leaves pressed flat all work beautifully. Let kids choose the material and design the layout entirely themselves.

Kids design and lay the entire floor path
Sunday — planting
7

Prepare the soil along both sides

Dig a shallow trench about 4 in deep and 6 in wide along the outside base of both rows. Fork the soil over and break up any clumps. Mix in a little compost if you have it. Water the trench before planting — moist soil helps seeds germinate faster.

Kids dig and fork their trench sections
8

Plant every seed

Runner beans go on one full side at 6 in intervals — push each seed about 2 in deep. Sweet peas, morning glories, and nasturtiums go on the other side in a natural mix. Cover with soil, firm down gently, and water generously. Runner bean and nasturtium seeds are large enough for even very small kids to handle confidently.

Kids plant every single seed — one of those moments they remember forever
9

Make the weekly growth chart

On a large piece of card, kids draw the tunnel outline and mark each planting spot with the seed type and their name. Each week they measure the tallest shoot and mark it with a different colour line. By week 8 the chart will be remarkable. Keep it going all summer. Frame it at the end of the season.

Kids design, run, and own the chart all summer

Hammer every stake in at least 12 in — non-negotiable

Shallow stakes pull out in wind and the whole tunnel collapses. Check every one for wobble after hammering. If the ground is very hard, pre-make a pilot hole with a metal spike. Five extra minutes per stake saves rebuilding after the first storm.

Water every day for the first three weeks

Seeds need consistent moisture to germinate. One missed day in hot weather can delay germination by a week. Set up a daily watering routine with the kids — it becomes a lovely ritual and they love watching for the first shoots.

Start runner beans inside two weeks early for a head start

Push runner bean seeds into small pots of compost indoors two weeks before the tunnel build. By planting weekend they'll already be 3–4 in tall seedlings. This gets the tunnel closing over about two weeks faster.

The tunnel needs to face the sun

All these climbing plants need full sun to thrive. Orient the tunnel so neither side is in full shade — ideally running roughly east to west so both sides get sun at different times of day.

Collect seeds at end of summer — year two costs nothing

Leave a few runner bean pods to dry fully on the plant. Shell and store in a paper envelope over winter. Let sweet pea and nasturtium seed heads dry on the plant, then collect. Next spring's entire tunnel costs zero in seeds. True cradle to cradle.

End of life — what to do with it

At the end of summer, pull all the stakes — compost or bonfire. Cut all plant material into short sections and pile onto the compost heap — it breaks down over winter into rich compost ready for next spring. Jute rope and twine compost completely. Collected seeds get planted again next year at zero cost. The whole structure feeds next year's garden. The most truly circular build on this entire list.
